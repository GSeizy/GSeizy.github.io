How to Build Your PC

This is it! You have (I hope) taken the necessary steps and precautions to carefully choose the parts to your PC. Go ahead and get them organized now and use the guide below to put it all together!

Step-by-Step Assembly

1. Prepare your workspace

A good workspace environment is clean, dust free, has good lighting, and a smooth, flat surface to work on. Remove the left, right, and front plates of your case.

(Handy Advice: Don't throw away any manuals or literature that come with your components. This is a generalized guide. Your individual component manuals may have certain recommendations or requirements as all manufacturers are different.)

2. Install the CPU

On your motherboard, locate the CPU socket. Open the bracket, align the CPU chip with the corresponding markers and gently place the chip in. DO NOT FORCE IT. CPU's are very fragile devices and if you bend the contact pins, it's over. Gently close the bracket back over the CPU until you hear a light click and you're done.

3. Install the CPU cooler

If not pre-applied on the CPU Cooler, apply thermal paste to the face of the CPU chip in an X pattern. Apply slowly and carefully but don't cake it on. Mount the cooler over top of the CPU chip and screw in the 4 screws at each corner of the cooler. Make sure it is secure and move on to Step 4.

4. Install the RAM

If you've correctly chosen a compatible motherboard, your RAM choice should line up nicely. Locate the RAM slots on the motherboard, open the clips, and gently insert the RAM sticks with light to medium pressure until the clips spring back. This will lock the sticks into place.

5. Install the case fans

If your case didn't come with enough fans or your preference is to add extras, now is the best time. Mount intake fans at the front and exhaust fans at the back or top. This keeps air flowing clean and in one direction so heat doesn't get trapped. It's easier to do this step now before the case gets crowded with other components.

6. Install the motherboard in the case

First and foremost, install the I/O shield in the back of the case. Once that is done, line up the motherboard with the standoffs (the threaded holes for the screws) and gently fasten each screw. Don't overtighten them or you risk damaging the motherboard.

7. Install the PSU

In the bottom of your chosen case, there should be a slot about the size of your chosen power supply unit. Insert the unit in gently in case it snags or doesn't fit properly. Route the 24 Pin ATX, EPS12V, PCIe, SATA, and Molex cables properly throughout the case. Once you have them routed to the proper places, use the zipties, or velcro cable straps to secure them together. Make sure they reach their proper connectors before you tighten them together so you don't have to stretch or redo them.

8. Install storage drives

If you chose SSD: 2.5in SATA will fit into a small drive tray on the rear side of the case and will connect to the SATA power and corresponding data cable from the motherboard. M.2 NVMe will connect directly on to the front of the motherboard in a small bracket that matches the size.

If you chose HDD: The bottom front of your case will have trays (or bays) where you will mount the disk and, again, run the proper cables to it.

9. Install the GPU

On the back of the case (below the I/O shield) remove the necessary slot covers. On the front of the motherboard, insert the GPU into the PCIe slot, and secure it with screws. AGAIN DO NOT FORCE IT Just like the CPU, you can easily bend the delicate pins of the PCIe or damage the motherboard. Depending on how large of a GPU you got, you may need a GPU support bracket or kickstand. Depending on the manufacturer (or individual GPU) you chose, you may or may not have one included. If the GPU is too heavy for the motherboard, this can cause flexing and connection issues. Make sure you have one if necessary.

(AAANNNDDD....in case you thought about it, no, do not go MacGuyver and make one yourself from a pencil or something. This increases risk of fire and other hazards. Do the smart thing and get a good one. You've already spent this much, spend the extra $20 and protect your investment.)

10. Connect all cables

Plug in the 24-pin motherboard power, CPU power, GPU power, and front panel connectors. (Hint: if the connector doesn't fit, chances are you have the wrong one. Many PC cables are meant to fit in one place to avoid this mistake. This is known as "keying.")

11. First boot

Double-check all (and I mean ALL) of your connections. If all looks good, give that power button that sweet first press. If it boots to BIOS, you're good to go to the next step! If something seems wrong, please consult our Help Page

12. Install Windows

Take out that USB drive loaded up with Windows. Once your PC posts to BIOS, plug in the USB drive and restart. The installer should boot automatically. If not, go back into the BIOS and change the boot order to prioritize the USB drive. From there, you'll want to follow the rest of the on-screen instructions to install Windows on your storage drive. Once complete, remove the USB and restart. Just like that you're done! Enjoy the fruits of your labor, you've earned it!
